Research · Bachelor Thesis · 2023

Empirical Findings on Strategic Partnerships between Cities, Municipal Companies and Private Startups

Format Bachelor Thesis
University Zeppelin University
Supervisor Chair of Public Management & Public Policy
Prof. Dr. Ulf Papenfuß
Subject Municipalities, municipal companies and startups
Year 2023

Abstract

To ensure a sovereign digital provision of services of general interest within the framework of SDG 11 (Sustainable Cities and Communities), municipalities increasingly enter into strategic partnerships with private startups and their own municipal companies. But what makes these partnerships work?

This thesis examines the role of trust as an enabling factor in cross-sector collaboration — investigating how trust is built, maintained and sometimes lost in partnerships between city administrations, municipal enterprises and digital startups.

Methods

The bachelor thesis employed a quantitative research design to analyse partnerships in the context of digital public service provision. A survey instrument was developed and distributed to practitioners across municipal administrations, municipal companies and startup organisations in Germany.

  • Survey-based data collection across public and private partnership actors
  • Correlation analysis between trust indicators and network performance measures
  • Statistical analysis of cooperation quality as a function of institutional trust

The analysis examined the relationship between different dimensions of trust — institutional, interpersonal and competence-based — and their effect on network performance and cooperation quality within strategic partnerships.

Implications & Personal Learning

The results indicate that trust is a central enabling factor for effective partnerships in digital public service provision. High levels of interpersonal trust between key actors were consistently associated with better coordination, greater willingness to share information and more durable collaboration.

Importantly, institutional trust — trust in the formal rules and procedures of the partnership — proved to be a necessary but insufficient condition. Without interpersonal trust, formal frameworks alone could not sustain productive collaboration over time.

This research shaped my understanding of governance not as a set of procedures but as a social practice. Effective urban governance — whether in digital services, spatial planning or public participation — depends on relationships built through repeated interaction, transparency and accountability. This insight has informed how I approach collaborative planning processes ever since.
