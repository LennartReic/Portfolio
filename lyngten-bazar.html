Lygten Bazar: Lived Multicultural Urbanism in Nørrebro

Format Exam Project
Cities, Culture & Politics
University Roskilde University
Supervisor David Pinder
Collaboration Lygten Bazar, Nørrebro
Co-Producer Gerald Reed Dolan
Programme Nordic Urban Planning Studies

About the Project

This video project was produced as part of the course Cities, Culture & Politics in the Nordic Urban Planning Studies programme at Roskilde University. The course examines how urban spaces are shaped by the intersection of culture, power and everyday life.

Our project focused on Lygten Bazar in Nørrebro, Copenhagen — a local market that functions as much more than a place to buy groceries. The bazar brings together diverse communities through the everyday rhythms of commerce, conversation and coexistence. It is a space where multiculturalism is not celebrated as a brand, but simply lived.

Approach

The video explores the bazar as a lived urban space rather than a branded or curated one. Rather than interviewing from a distance, we spent time inside the bazar, filming and speaking with vendors and visitors to understand the social fabric that holds the market together.

Through filming and interviews, the project also revealed tensions surrounding visibility, gentrification and the fragility of multicultural urban spaces in cities undergoing rapid transformation. The bazar exists in a neighbourhood under increasing development pressure — its future is not guaranteed.

Due to the intimate nature of the space and the trust required to film, the process was as much about relationship-building as it was about production. The result is a document of a particular urban texture — one that formal planning rarely captures.

Personal Learning

This project deepened my interest in ethnographic and qualitative methods as tools for understanding urban life beyond what statistics and masterplans reveal. The bazar showed me how much planning misses when it focuses only on form — the social infrastructure of a place is equally, if not more, important.

It also raised questions I continue to carry: Whose city is being planned? For whom do urban spaces become legible, welcoming, or threatening? These are not abstract questions — they are answered every day in places like Lygten Bazar.
