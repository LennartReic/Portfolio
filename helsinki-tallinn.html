Research · Group Project · 2024

Two Branches of the Same Tree: Rethinking the Helsinki–Tallinn Twin City Strategy

Format Group Project
University Roskilde University
Supervisor Anette Stenslund
Department of People and Technology
Cases City of Helsinki
City of Tallinn
Year 2024
Programme Nordic Urban Planning Studies

Abstract

This project examines the Helsinki–Tallinn twin city strategy through the lens of planning practices and collaborative governance — asking how the relationship between the two capitals is perceived from both above (institutional actors, policy documents, planning frameworks) and below (civil society, residents, cross-border communities).

The twin city concept presents an intriguing planning challenge: two sovereign capitals in two countries, separated by 80 km of sea, attempting to coordinate urban development, mobility and economic policy as if they formed a shared urban region. What does it mean to plan across such a divide?

Methods

The project employed a qualitative, multi-method research design to analyse the Helsinki–Tallinn twin city strategy from both institutional and everyday perspectives.

  • Document analysis of official twin city strategy papers, planning frameworks and joint declarations from both municipalities
  • Semi-structured interviews with planning practitioners and civil society representatives in Helsinki and Tallinn
  • Ethnographic observation on the Viking Line ferry connecting the two cities — the primary physical link between the twin cities and a space of cross-border everyday life
  • Discourse analysis comparing institutional narratives with lived experiences of cross-border mobility

The multi-method approach allowed us to hold institutional ambitions and everyday realities in tension — and to ask where they diverge most significantly.

Implications & Personal Learning

The findings suggest that twin city strategies risk remaining symbolic when they are not grounded in the everyday practices and needs of cross-border communities. The strategy documents articulate a shared urban vision, but the mechanisms for translating this vision into coordinated planning action remain underdeveloped.

A recurring tension emerged between the branding logic of twin city discourse — which emphasises growth, competitiveness and attractiveness — and the planning logic needed to address housing affordability, commuter mobility and social integration across national boundaries.

The ferry as a field site proved unexpectedly rich: it made visible the daily rhythms of cross-border life that rarely appear in strategic documents — workers, students, shoppers and families moving between cities for reasons entirely unrelated to any twin city strategy.

This project deepened my interest in transnational and cross-border planning as an emerging field — one where the tools of conventional urban planning must be adapted to contexts of divided jurisdiction, different welfare systems and competing political priorities.
