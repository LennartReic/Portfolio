Research · Humboldt Project · 2023

Effects of Collaborative Innovation on the Design of City Apps

Format Humboldt Project
University Zeppelin University
Supervisor Chair of Public Management & Public Policy
Prof. Dr. Ulf Papenfuß
Cases City of Karlsruhe
City of Solingen
City of Wolfsburg
HEAG Darmstadt
Year 2023

Abstract

There is growing competition between platform companies and urban actors to provide digital public services. This research examines how municipalities develop city apps — and whether collaborative innovation processes between public institutions, municipal companies and private partners lead to better design outcomes.

The study asks: Does the governance structure of app development affect the functionality and user-centricity of the final product? And what does this mean for municipal digital sovereignty?

Methods

The thesis applied a qualitative research design focused on understanding governance and collaboration dynamics in digital public service development. Four cases were selected to represent different organisational models: city-led, partnership-based, utility-led and outsourced development.

  • Semi-structured expert interviews with project leads, IT officers and policy makers across the four cities
  • Document analysis of procurement records, project documentation and app store reviews
  • Comparative case analysis across governance structures and design outcomes

The framework drew on collaborative governance theory and digital public service literature to evaluate how participation structures, coordination mechanisms and accountability arrangements shape what cities are able to build — and for whom.

Implications & Personal Learning

The findings imply that the success of city apps depends more on governance capacity and coordination than on technical capability. Cities that integrated citizen feedback early and maintained cross-departmental ownership produced apps that were more consistently used and better adapted to local needs.

Conversely, highly outsourced development processes often resulted in generic products with low adoption — built to specification, but not to use.

This research deepened my understanding of how public institutions navigate digital transformation — and the structural barriers that prevent municipalities from acting as coherent digital actors. It also sharpened my interest in the intersection of governance design and spatial digital infrastructure, which has become increasingly relevant in contemporary urban planning.
