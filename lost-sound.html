Lost & Sound Festival

Format Festival (Non-profit)

Brodowin, Brandenburg

Overview

Lost & Sound is a fully biological music and arts festival that takes place on the grounds of an organic farm in Brodowin, Brandenburg. It is conceived as an immersive space rather than a conventional event: there is no clear stage hierarchy, no VIP area, no branded merchandise. The festival is a temporary city — with circulation, gathering places, thresholds and edges.

Founded by five students and realised through the commitment of many volunteers, the festival aims to give small and emerging artists a stage and to create an environment where self-expression, connection and sustainability are lived values rather than marketing claims.

By 2025, Lost & Sound has grown to 500 visitors, with three stages and a wide range of informal spaces in between — places to dance, to pause, to meet others, or simply to get lost for a while. The festival balances collective programming with intentional emptiness.

Festival grounds at Brodowin, 2025

What Was My Role?

My role at Lost and Sound was Site Planner. I was responsible for translating many individual ideas into a coherent spatial plan that could be built, communicated and inhabited by 500 people over three days.

Rather than directing creatives or craftsmen, my task was to facilitate collaboration. I brought together volunteers, builders, artists and organisers — coordinated materials and workflows, mediated between conflicting spatial ideas, and translated concept sketches into buildable instructions.

The work started around six months before the festival with concept meetings, where ideas for stages, lounges, art installations and circulation routes were developed. Over time, these were consolidated into a site plan that balanced atmosphere, safety, flow and budget.

One of the most challenging aspects was organising materials within a very limited budget. Relying largely on reused materials, second-hand items and donated resources required creative problem-solving and close coordination with local suppliers and volunteers.

The Snake Bench

A project I am particularly proud of is a large bench structure I designed for the festival: the Snake Bench. It was conceived as a flexible social infrastructure — a winding, continuous seating form that creates intimate clusters within a larger open space, encourages spontaneous gathering and works equally well for one person or twenty.

The bench was built from reclaimed timber by a team of volunteers over two days on-site. It became one of the most used spaces at the festival — a reminder that the most lasting spatial interventions are often the simplest ones.

Personal Learning

Planning Lost & Sound was among the most direct urban planning experiences I have had. The festival is, in miniature, everything that cities must negotiate: diverse users, limited resources, competing interests, safety requirements and the need to leave room for the unexpected.

It reinforced my belief that good spatial planning is above all about facilitating — not determining. The best outcomes came not from a perfect plan, but from creating the conditions in which people could shape the space themselves.
