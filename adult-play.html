Research · Group Project · 2025

In Defense of Adult Play in Public Spaces

Format Group Project
University Roskilde University
Supervisor Jonas Larsen
Department of People and Technology
Cases Guldbergs Plads
Fælledparken, Copenhagen
Year 2025
Programme Nordic Urban Planning Studies

Abstract

This research examines how public urban spaces in Copenhagen can be planned to encourage adult play. Grounded in Practice Theory, the project investigates the social, spatial and cultural conditions that enable or inhibit adults from engaging in play in public settings.

The study develops "The Right to the Playground" as a planning principle for equitable access to playful public spaces — arguing that play is not a frivolous add-on, but a legitimate and underserved dimension of urban life.

Methods

The project applied a qualitative, theory-driven research design grounded in Practice Theory to analyse how play is performed, inhibited or facilitated in different urban contexts.

Guldbergs Plads and a grass field in Fælledparken were selected to compare different spatial and social conditions. Empirical data was collected through systematic field observations, ethnographic mapping, and qualitative interviews with users of both spaces.

The analytical framework focused on the interplay between spatial design, cultural meanings and social norms — examining not just what people do in these spaces, but what prevents them from playing.

Implications & Personal Learning

The findings suggest that adult play is less constrained by spatial absence than by social norms, stigma and perceived legitimacy. Natural environments and flexible spaces support adult play, while rigid or overly programmed design tends to suppress it.

A key conclusion was the entanglement of spatial design and cultural meanings: public spaces should support a range of playful competences, abilities and seasonal uses to ensure inclusion rather than exclusion.

Based on our findings, we formulated a planner's checklist — a practical framework for designing spaces that actively invite adults to play:

  • Who Do You Want?
    Planning for everyone doesn't work when planning for play. Choose a clear target group and design playfully for them.
  • Look & Fit
    Adjust colours, materials and aesthetics to adult tastes — not children's equipment transposed to adult scale.
  • People Magnet
    Attract and keep other people as social infrastructure. Passive presence reduces the stigma of playing alone.
  • Beginner Friendly
    Make it easy to join in. Include elements that work for low physical and mental confidence or ability.
  • Challenge Time
    Match the target group's competences and enable competition or progression on different levels.
  • Nature
    Nature appeals to most people. Use seasonality playfully and integrate climate adaptation features.
  • Open Space
    Leave room for unplanned, spontaneous play. Not everything needs a programme.
  • No Sweat Without Fun
    Offer fitness potential, but keep fun as the primary focus — not the other way around.
  • No Fun Without Risk
    Add exciting elements. Play involves risk, but it should not be dangerous.
  • Outside the Ordinary
    Create an environment that feels different from everyday life, making it easier to step into a playful mode.
  • Be Aware of Kids
    Children can spark play for parents and playful adults, but can also dominate an area and discourage others.
  • The Right to the Playground
    Some groups may be marginalised. Observe who uses the space, how it develops, and who feels welcome.
